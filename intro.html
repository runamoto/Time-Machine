Let’s be clear, this was not my first time having a go at coding. I took a computer science class and tried to teach myself javascript all last summer…Not to mention my Graphic Design 2 course was almost entirely composed of having to learn code. I’m going on record to say that although those classes, websites and projects were all near and dear to my heart, I never really learned how to code from them. Most of it was me asking chatGPT and my friends for help. That all was about to change with this project. “Time Machine is project where you will track some aspect of your subject at a time interval of your choosing”. A million different themes stuck out in my head: I could do something about the history of the ‘Aurat march’ movement in Pakistan, or about the reign of Zia Ul Haq that Arabized the country of Pakistan and it’s impacts seen in society today. So many topics that I was itching to research and bring awareness towards…But in doing those things, I feared that I would once again follow that relentless cycle of relying on chat GPT to do all the coding for me whilst I focused on the design aspect. But that wasn’t going to happen. In the end, I decided to employ a more personal project, something that would leave a lasting impression on not only my perception of programming and code but also on my skill set.
My project is a Time Machine that takes you through my progression understanding code in the 5 weeks 4 days we had for this. It is a testament for my to look back onto and see how far I was able to make it in such a short amount of time, granted that I would have never even thought I would get this far. As a woman of colour I was never encouraged to explore the bounds of computer science, it was always framed as a mans job, something that requires logic, reasoning and ratiocination. Too much math for a girl like me. And until a while ago, I internalized this. All the men around me were the only ones who could code—Heck, even at OCAD, the only people who I know could code well were men. Even with this knowledge of internalized disdain toward code, I couldn’t break out of the shell. Until I did.
Now that this project is over, I still want to continue to expand my coding skills, learn more advanced java script and play around with p5.js to create tools that help me in my practice and to create interesting generative works that I would have never envisioned myself doing.
Recently I was made aware that my work lacks a refined ‘quality’. To that I say fuck you, I’m in graphic design undergrad at OCAD University, what do you expect. But also, it’s not that my work lacks what some people regard as commendable or exemplary, it lacks marketability, and that’s something I’m okay with.
